## Quick Answer

Pairs trading bets on relative value: buy a beaten-down stock, short a outperforming one (usually competitors). Example: buy FDX (Fed Ex down 15%), short UPS (United Parcel up 10%). When gap closes: both sides profit. Target: 15-30% combined move. Works in any market direction (market neutral).


## Introduction

Pairs trading is the sophisticated trader's secret. Instead of betting on market direction, you bet on relative value. This removes market risk and captures alpha. This guide covers everything.

## Trading Strategies


### Strategy 1: Same Sector Pairs

**Description:** Buy the weaker company in a sector, short the stronger one.

**How It Works:**
In banking: JPM up 20%, BAC down 5%. Relative underperformance suggests BAC will catch up. Long 100 BAC, short 100 JPM.

**Key Indicators:**
Relative performance, Correlation, Technical levels

**Real Example:**
Tech: NVDA up 50%, AMD down 5% (outlier). Long 100 AMD, short 50 NVDA. When AMD rises to match NVDA: $2000+ profit.


### Strategy 2: ETF Pairs Trading

**Description:** Pair related ETFs that historically correlate but recently diverged.

**How It Works:**
SPY and QQQ normally move together. If SPY down 5% but QQQ only down 2%: QQQ is stronger. Long QQQ, short SPY (or short both, but SPY more).

**Key Indicators:**
Correlation divergence, Relative strength, Technical support

**Real Example:**
QQQ/SPY ratio high = QQQ strong. Short SPY, long QQQ. Capture 200-300 points when ratio normalizes.


### Strategy 3: Statistical Arbitrage Pairs

**Description:** Use mean reversion on pairs: when correlation breaks, trade the bounce back.

**How It Works:**
GLD and GDX (gold ETF and miners ETF) highly correlated. When GDX diverges significantly: mean revert. If GDX way up: short GDX, long GLD.

**Key Indicators:**
Correlation coefficient, Z-score divergence, Technical levels

**Real Example:**
GDX way outperforming GLD: short 100 GDX, long 100 GLD. When correlation reverts: both positions close profitably.


## Risk Management & Position Sizing

Risk 1-2% per pairs trade. Position size: equal dollar amount or delta-neutral. Hold until correlation reverts or technical breakdown. Pairs trades: typically 5-30 days.

**Critical Rules:**
- Never risk more than your predetermined % per trade
- Use hard stop losses on every position
- Exit immediately if market structure breaks
- Account for spread/commissions in profit calculations
- Adjust position size based on volatility

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

Traders often fall into these pitfalls when using this strategy:

1. **Pairing unrelated companies (weak correlation)**
2. **Using old historical correlations (may have changed)**
3. **Asymmetric position sizing (dollar amounts must match)**
4. **Holding through earnings (can widen gap)**
5. **Ignoring fundamental differences (why are they different?)**
6. **Over-leveraging (both sides can fail)**

## Frequently Asked Questions


### How do you find good pairs to trade?

Look for: (1) same sector/industry (competitors), (2) high historical correlation > 0.7, (3) current divergence from average spread, (4) recent fundamental differences explaining divergence.


### What's the difference between pairs trading and hedging?

Hedging: protects against directional loss. Pairs: exploits relative mispricing. Hedging: reduced profit potential. Pairs: captures alpha from both sides.


### How much can pairs traders make?

Conservative: 5-10% annualized (low volatility). Aggressive: 15-30% annualized (higher leverage). Depends on pair selection and divergence size.


### Can pairs trading work with crypto?

Yes: BTC/ETH pairs, or crypto/ETF pairs. Crypto pairs more volatile: gains/losses faster. Use tighter stops. Crypto correlation changes seasonally.


### What's a correlation coefficient and why does it matter?

Correlation = how much two assets move together (-1 to +1). +1 = perfect correlation (same movement). Above +0.7 = strong pairs candidates. Below +0.5 = weak pairs (unpredictable results).
